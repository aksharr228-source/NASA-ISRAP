NASA ISRAP

Multi-scale, Multiplatform Sensing · Machine-Learning Prediction Models · Visualization Framework · Air Quality Decisions

Making Earth livable by mapping San Antonio's air, one bus route at a time.

ISRAP is a NASA-affiliated research initiative at UTSA confronting San Antonio's biggest environmental challenge: air pollution. We mount mobile sensing platforms on UTSA buses to measure pollutants like PM2.5, PM10, ozone (O₃), NO₂, and CO in real time across the city. These pollutants are linked to respiratory disease, cancer, and greater vulnerability to infections like the flu. We turn that data into action with machine-learning prediction models built for the SATX community, a visualization framework that makes the data clear, and evidence to guide real air-quality policy decisions.

Onboard sensor payload
  • PurpleAir · PM2.5 / PM10
  • Alphasense OX-B431 · O₃ + NO₂
  • Alphasense NO2-B43F · O₃ + NO₂
  • Alphasense CO-B4 · CO
  • Sensirion SCD40 · CO₂
  • Sensirion SHT45 · Temp / Humidity
  • u-blox NEO-M8N · GPS

Student Highlights

Built by students, for the city

At its core, ISRAP is a student-led effort built around one idea: turn UTSA's transit buses into a moving network of air-quality sensors. The project brings together students from across the university, each contributing the strengths of their own major so the work spans hardware, data, and public health alike.

  • Cybersecurity & Data Science: securing the data pipeline and protecting the pollution data the sensors collect.
  • Public Health: interpreting the readings to uncover the real health impact of the pollutants we measure, which can be genuinely harmful.
  • Mechanical Engineering: designing the sensor hardware, including the Raspberry Pi enclosure, and mounting the units onto the buses.

Every sensor package is assembled and tested in-house before it goes anywhere near a bus. Once validated, the enclosures are mounted on UTSA buses that travel across San Antonio, including the route from the UTSA Main Campus to the UTSA Downtown Campus, collecting live pollution data along the way.
